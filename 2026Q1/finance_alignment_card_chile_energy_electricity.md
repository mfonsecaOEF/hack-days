# Finance Alignment Card

## 1. Project
- Country: Chile
- Project name: Municipal Buildings Electrification and Solar Retrofit Program for Chilean Communes
- Project type: Mitigation
- Short description: A municipal-scale program to reduce electricity demand and fossil fuel use in public buildings through energy-efficiency retrofits, rooftop solar under distributed-generation rules, replacement of fossil-based heating systems with efficient electric systems, and local capacity-building for long-term energy management.

## 2. SDG Alignment
- Primary SDGs: SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy), SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities), SDG 13 (Climate Action), SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure), SDG 17 (Partnerships for the Goals)
- Why these SDGs: The project improves access to cleaner electricity solutions, modernizes municipal infrastructure, reduces emissions from public buildings, and requires coordination between municipalities, national agencies, technical providers, and local communities. It also fits well with OEF's HIAP and CityCatalyst logic of moving from climate assessment toward prioritized and implementable mitigation actions.
- Strength of alignment: 5

## 3. NDC / National Policy Alignment
- Relevant NDC priorities: Chile's NDC 2025 update is framed as a ten-year roadmap toward a carbon-neutral, resilient, and sustainable country. It strengthens the role of public-private collaboration and regional and local governments, and includes instruments such as the social cost of carbon in public investment evaluation and stronger price signals for emissions.
- Relevant national policy or sector priorities: Chile's Energy Efficiency Law and National Energy Efficiency Plan 2022-2026 identify buildings, public-use buildings, energy efficiency, and smart cities as core areas for action. Chile also continues to promote distributed generation through net-billing and to deepen its long-term energy transition agenda.
- Why this project fits: A municipal electrification and solar retrofit program directly supports Chile's mitigation agenda by lowering energy use, reducing operational emissions in public buildings, and strengthening municipal capacity to implement cleaner electricity solutions. It is especially relevant because Chile's climate and energy policy framework explicitly gives local governments an important implementation role.
- Strength of alignment: 5

## 4. Finance Context
- Why this could be financeable: Chile has one of the strongest sustainable-finance architectures in the region for a project like this. A municipal electricity and retrofit program can be framed as a green public investment with measurable mitigation benefits, energy savings, and replicability across communes.
- Relevant public finance / budget / climate finance signals: Signal 1: Chile's Ministry of Finance has an active sovereign ESG bond program, beginning with green bonds and expanding through sustainable and sustainability-linked bond frameworks. Signal 2: Chile officially published its environmentally sustainable economic activities taxonomy in May 2025 to guide public and private investment and improve access to sustainable finance. Signal 3: Chile's Energy Efficiency Law projects major economic savings and emissions reductions by 2030, which strengthens the case for efficiency-focused investment in buildings and electricity use. Signal 4: Chile's distributed generation and net-billing framework already supports rooftop solar and self-consumption in buildings.
- GFLAC-style observation: Using GFLAC's lens, this project is strong because it points financial flows toward low-carbon and climate-aligned activity rather than carbon-intensive infrastructure. The main question is not whether it sounds green, but whether the investment is transparently aligned with a low-carbon transition, includes social safeguards, and can be tracked as part of broader sustainable-finance priorities.
- Strength of alignment: 5

## 5. Governance Readiness
- Community participation included? Yes, but it should be strengthened in project design.
- Civil society role: Civil society and local technical organizations could help identify priority public buildings, support energy-literacy campaigns, and monitor whether the benefits of lower energy costs and better public infrastructure are reaching underserved communities.
- Youth governance role: Youth should be included through a local energy transition council or municipal youth climate committee that participates in building selection, awareness campaigns, and basic monitoring of project outcomes.
- Main governance gaps: The concept still needs a clear municipal governance structure, a participation process for selecting target buildings, a plan for maintenance and operations, and clearer rules for how youth and community input would influence decisions rather than only observe them.
- Strength of alignment: 4

## 6. Overall Assessment
- Overall funding narrative: Strong
- Main strengths: The project has clear fit with Chile's mitigation, energy-efficiency, and local implementation priorities; it aligns naturally with multiple SDGs; and it sits inside a country context with strong sustainable-finance instruments and policy signals.
- Main risks: The concept still needs clearer targeting of communes and building types, a stronger implementation model for procurement and maintenance, better definition of financing structure at municipal level, and a more concrete participation model for communities and youth.
- What would make this more finance-ready: Define the first pilot communes, specify the building portfolio and retrofit package, estimate energy and emissions savings, identify the likely financing vehicle or blended-finance route, and formalize a governance model that includes municipal leadership, civil society, and youth participation.
