# Finance Alignment Card

## 1. Project
- Country: Brazil
- Project name: Community-Led Flood Resilience Green Corridors for Porto Alegre Peripheries
- Project type: Adaptation
- Short description: A neighborhood-scale nature-based solutions project for flood-prone peripheral areas of Porto Alegre combining rain gardens, bioswales, permeable surfaces, pocket parks, and community stewardship with a formal youth governance component for monitoring and co-management.

## 2. SDG Alignment
- Primary SDGs: SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities), SDG 13 (Climate Action), SDG 6 (Clean Water and Sanitation), SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities), SDG 17 (Partnerships for the Goals)
- Why these SDGs: The project strengthens urban resilience, reduces climate risk, improves stormwater management, targets underserved peripheral communities, and depends on cross-sector delivery between local government, civil society, and community groups. This is also consistent with the OEF CityCatalyst direction of helping cities move from assessment to finance-ready projects.
- Strength of alignment: 5

## 3. NDC / National Policy Alignment
- Relevant NDC priorities: Brazil's Second NDC relies on an updated national Climate Plan with a National Adaptation Strategy, sixteen sectoral adaptation plans, and a transversal strategy covering just transition, socioeconomic impacts, means of implementation, and monitoring/transparency. It also emphasizes implementation across federal, state, and municipal levels.
- Relevant national policy or sector priorities: Brazil's Climate Plan is framed around sustainable development, climate justice, and adaptation of human and natural systems. The NDC explicitly highlights participatory planning with civil society, the private sector, and the scientific community, and recognizes municipal implementation as fundamental.
- Why this project fits: A flood resilience NbS project in Porto Alegre directly supports adaptation, urban resilience, and climate justice goals while fitting the municipal implementation logic described in Brazil's NDC. The project is especially strong because it combines resilience infrastructure with participation by communities and youth rather than treating governance as an afterthought.
- Strength of alignment: 5

## 4. Finance Context
- Why this could be financeable: Brazil already has public and blended-finance signals that match this type of project. BNDES's Fundo Clima line for resilient and sustainable urban development supports eligible interventions such as permeable surfaces, water reuse, and sustainable urban infrastructure, while the Brazil Climate and Ecological Transformation Investment Platform is meant to connect climate plans to investment pipelines across key sectors, including nature-based solutions.
- Relevant public finance / budget / climate finance signals: Signal 1: BNDES Fundo Clima has a resilient urban development line with financing for sustainable urban works and climate-relevant infrastructure. Signal 2: Brazil's Ministry of Finance launched the Brazil Climate and Ecological Transformation Investment Platform to mobilize public and private capital into concrete climate and ecological transformation projects. Signal 3: UNDP's SDG budget-tagging guidance supports the idea that ministries of finance and subnational governments can map spending against SDG-aligned projects rather than relying only on narrative alignment.
- GFLAC-style observation: Brazil has strong climate-finance momentum, but the public-finance picture is still mixed. GFLAC reports that Brazil still receives much more revenue from carbon-intensive activities than from sustainable finance and allocates more budget to carbon-intensive sectors than to sustainable initiatives. That means the project should be framed as a high-quality adaptation investment with measurable resilience outcomes, not just a good idea.
- Strength of alignment: 4

## 5. Governance Readiness
- Community participation included? Yes
- Civil society role: Civil society organizations should co-design the intervention, support outreach in peripheral neighborhoods, help identify priority sites, and co-manage maintenance and accountability after installation.
- Youth governance role: Youth should have explicit seats in the governance structure through a local youth climate or resilience committee, with responsibilities for participatory monitoring, risk mapping, and stewardship of public green infrastructure.
- Main governance gaps: No named local partner is yet attached; the decision-making process between municipality and community is still undefined; the participation budget is not specified; and no formal monitoring mechanism has yet been assigned to youth or civil society actors.
- Strength of alignment: 4

## 6. Overall Assessment
- Overall funding narrative: Strong
- Main strengths: The project has clear fit with Brazil's adaptation and climate-justice framing, maps well to multiple SDGs, matches emerging Brazilian finance instruments for resilient urban development, and aligns closely with OEF's existing NbS and project-preparation direction in Brazil.
- Main risks: The concept still needs a named municipal sponsor, a credible capital and operating budget, clearer evidence on who will maintain the assets, and a more specific explanation of how youth and community governance will work in practice.
- What would make this more finance-ready: Add a municipal implementation partner, define target neighborhoods and assets, estimate capex and opex, specify resilience metrics, identify one financing pathway to test first, and formalize the governance model with civil society and youth participation roles.
